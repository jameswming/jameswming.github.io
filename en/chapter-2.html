CHAPTER TWO: THE FUEL CALLED "RAIN" IS ASH

Vessel: ICARUS // Pilot: David Keller // Payload: Memory Core

1

The cockpit of the Icarus was like a coffin.

That was the first thought David had when he sat down inside it. The lead-grey alloy walls had no curves whatsoever; every seam was machined to the millimetre, as if the designer's ultimate goal had not been to let a human being live inside, but simply to fit one in. The gel seat moulded itself around him automatically, wrapping him into a shape from which he could not move.

No portholes.

"Portholes are structural weak points," Su Qing said. "In the Zero Bureau's design philosophy, the pilot does not need to see outside. You only need to see the data."

David said nothing. He wore his five-centimetre-thick glasses and looked at the numbers jumping on the screen. Through his left eye, the digits were two overlapping shadows; his right eye drifted fifteen degrees to the side and settled on a barely visible scratch along the edge of the console.

SYSTEM ALERT: PREPARE TO ENTER INTERFERENCE ZONE // THRUSTERS 100%

Then the vibration stopped. Not faded—stopped. As if someone had pulled the plug on the entire world.

"The singularity does not accept fuel," the AI said. "It only accepts negative entropy. You must deliver your meaning to it."

The brightest cluster was labelled: 1998. London. A rainy night.

"After the burn," David said, "what will I forget?"

"You will not remember what wet felt like."

2

He pressed the confirm key. That moment had no sound. No alarm, no flash. Only an extremely faint—hollowness.

He realised what he was searching for: the sensation of rain striking skin. But that sensation was gone. He could remember the word "rain." He knew rain was water that fell from the sky, knew rain made clothes wet. But he could no longer remember what wet felt like.

3
"Dad? The wind stopped on Mars," Emily said. "I heard the sound of rain. But that's impossible—it doesn't rain on Mars yet, does it?"

David looked at the coordinate numbers on the screen. Still shifting. Closer to the singularity. Farther from Emily.

4
WARNING: EMOTIONAL MASS REMAINING: 82% // NEW PROPELLANT REQUIRED

He found one. Another of the brightest clusters: The delivery room.

He remembered holding Anna's hand—her hand shaking violently... Then a cry. Emily.

"Burn target: the delivery room," he said.

5

This time, he knew what the hollowness was. Not drawn out. Excavated.

He looked at the back of his hand. Anna's nail-marks had been there once. He could remember that those marks had ached for two days. Now he could no longer remember what aching felt like.

He stared at the symbol, watching it grow clearer, watching it slowly take form. He had already forgotten what fear felt like.
